Scenarios: How I Operate

Five scenarios. Five structured responses. Diagnose before acting. Align before executing. Measure before claiming success.

A

Shifting Priorities and Leadership Changes

▼

My first action in any leadership transition is 1:1s with every team member before any strategy session. I need to understand what was in flight, what was working, and what was quietly not working before I arrived. I consult with my new manager to align on what the team is expected to deliver and within what timeframe. I then share a direction that explicitly incorporates what I have learned from the team — not just what I think — so the team recognises their input in the plan. I halt any non-essential A/B tests immediately and focus the first 30 days on 2–3 revenue metrics the whole team can track. Clarity and stability in a transition period are more valuable than speed.

B

A Strong Performer Who Is Difficult to Collaborate With

▼

I lead with their results, not the behaviour. In a private 1:1, I acknowledge what they have built and then ask directly how they feel the collaboration is going — both with peers and cross-functional teams. Most people who are difficult to collaborate with are not aware of the full impact. I offer concrete tools: structured communication frameworks, joint decision threads, and a shared workspace for in-progress work. If the behaviour continues after two structured conversations with clear expectations set, I explore whether an individual contributor track is a better fit — some people produce exceptional work but are not suited to team dynamics. I would not let this affect the team's output while the situation is being addressed.

C

Growth Has Plateaued and the Team Feels Stuck

▼

I run a structured internal challenge: 48 hours, every team member proposes one format, one channel, or one audience segment we have not yet tested. I use AI research tools — competitor teardowns, social listening, app review mining — to surface signals the team may have missed. I also do my own personal audit: what assumptions have we been operating on for the longest without questioning? The unlock for a plateau is almost always a format, channel, or audience not yet tested — not more budget into the same channels. Once the team generates ideas, I pick the one with the highest expected value and lowest execution cost, and we test it within two weeks. Fast iteration on the edge of the current strategy breaks plateaus.

D

Cross-Functional Priorities Conflict

▼

My first step is to understand the other team's priorities before advocating for mine. In most priority conflicts, both teams are rational — they just have different success metrics. I build a business impact comparison: here is the revenue impact of my request, here is what I understand the impact of your request to be. I propose an urgency tagging system: Blocker (resolves within 24 hours), 3-Day (business impact materialising within 72 hours), Low (important but not time-sensitive). I frame all asks as joint efforts: "What would it take to move this forward together?" — not "Why is this blocked?" I escalate only if the business impact is demonstrably significant and the conversation has stalled.

E

A Team Member Not Hitting ROI Targets

▼

A UA manager on our Casino app was consistently missing ROAS targets across Google UAC campaigns. Before any conversation, I ran a full account audit: campaign structure, bid strategy, creative performance, and campaign age distribution. I identified a specific root cause: learning phase timing. New campaigns were being assessed and paused before Google's algorithm had accumulated sufficient conversion signal. The manager was optimising too early, killing campaigns before they could learn. I ran twice-weekly review sessions for four weeks, showing the data at each step — not delivering instructions. Target: +15% ROAS improvement. Outcome: +20% improvement in 4 weeks. That manager is now our strongest Google UAC operator.
